Part-3 product proposal · Blake Parsons · 2026-06-12

Finding what broke
is table stakes.
The job is moving the
business outcome.

Signal, LangSmith Engine, and Raindrop all find agent failures now — that's table stakes. The unclaimed job is the one that matters: making the agent better for the business, not just better-behaved. An eval score is only a proxy for the agent's true objective function — the business outcome it exists to move. Arize should instrument that outcome, rank every issue by what fixing it is worth, route the decision to whoever can ship it, and close it only when the metric actually moved.

first, the altitude · a hierarchy of evals

Not every eval failure is a bug to fix.

Eval failures stack on a hierarchy, and the rung decides what you do about them — patch it, or decide about it. The bottom is non-discretionary: did the agent run, and was it correct. Nobody convenes a meeting about a hallucination — you just fix it. Climb above that and the failures stop being bugs: the agent is reliable and accurate but lacks a tool, a permission, the context, or runs into a policy. Whether to extend or augment the agent — and how far — isn't a fix; it's a discretionary call someone owns, set by the business objective.

RungThe questionExample failureWhat you do with it
1 · Reliable & accurateDoes it run, and is it right?tool errors, malformed output, timeouts, hallucination, a wrong or ungrounded answerJust fix it. Must-fix, not a decision — broken or wrong, you solve it.
2 · Capable & in scope ▸ the proposal lives hereDoes it have what it needs to do the right thing?missing tool, missing permission, missing context, a policy that blocks correct reasoningFrame a decision. Discretionary — a tradeoff someone owns and records.
3 · Good experienceIs it a good experience?too verbose, off-tone, misses user frustrationTune — usually a product / design call.
4 · ExploratoryWhat don't we know yet?new intents, drift, unexplained spikesInvestigate.

The real cut is must-fix vs. discretionary. The obvious failures — a hallucination, a misfiring tool — are must-fix and table stakes; Signal, LangSmith Engine, and Raindrop already find them. This proposal is about the rung above: failures that pass every must-fix eval and still cost the business, where extending the agent is a call someone owns rather than a bug an engineer quietly ships. Up there, an eval stops being a health check and becomes a proxy for a business outcome — which is the thesis, and everything below builds on this climb.

what's solved, and what isn't

Finding the issue is table stakes. The business outcome isn't.

Above that line is where the value is — and it's unclaimed. Arize launched Signal this spring; LangSmith Engine and Raindrop shipped the same shape — they cluster must-fix failures into ranked issues with proposed fixes. What none of them owns is the discretionary work: making the agent better for the business. Three gaps sit between a found issue and a moved metric:

gap 1 · the objective function
An eval scores the agent, not the business.

Evals measure LLM behavior — was the turn grounded, was it correct. That's a proxy. It can read green while the business objective fails: a support eval can rate every turn "correct" and the order still never gets delivered. Nothing today ranks issues by the true objective function — the outcome the agent exists to move.

gap 2 · the decision
The fixes that matter live outside the repo.

The expensive failures aren't prompt diffs — they're a missing capability, a missing permission, a blocking policy, owned by someone who isn't the engineer. Getting that decided and implemented is the bottleneck. Today the handoff is a Slack screenshot; the decision stalls and nothing tracks why.

gap 3 · the proof
Nothing tracks the balanced outcome.

Ship → re-measure → attribute is manual everywhere. And no product checks the change against the balanced objective — did the north-star move without tripping a counter-metric — which is what tells you whether to keep the change or roll it back.

Closing those three is how Arize stops being the place you watch your agent and becomes the place you improve its business impact. And it's buildable on the self-serve tier — where the developers this assignment asks about actually live, and where no automated improvement loop reaches them today.

the thesis

An eval is an approximation of the true objective function. The objective function is a business outcome.

Every eval is a stand-in for what you actually want — and the gap between "the eval passed" and "the business won" is exactly where agents quietly fail. Observability that ends at the LLM boundary can describe the agent; only observability grounded in the real outcome can improve it. Arize should own the loop from outcome → objective-ranked issue → decision made → verified lift, and become accountable for whether the agent got better for the business — not just informed about what it did.

◆ why Arize, why now

The bigger value unlock isn't a better health monitor — it's moving up the hierarchy. Once evals confirm the agent is functional, the platform that watched it the whole way is the one positioned to drive the next decision: what to change, what it's worth, and whether it worked. That turns Arize from the place you watch the agent into the place you improve the business outcome — the larger payoff for the customer. And the timing is right: Signal validated the direction (the "AI Factory" arc is now Arize's flagship), and the bottleneck moved from building to deciding — coding agents made fixes cheap to draft; what's expensive now is getting the change decided, shipped by the right owner, and proven.

Neutrality is the leverage that makes it defensible. An outcome convention on OpenInference is a standard only the neutral player can credibly propose — anyone else's is a framework feature. And by leading here, Arize gets to steer how outcomes get implemented across the ecosystem — the same position that made OpenInference stick. That's a real edge, but it's secondary: the point is customer value first; setting the standard is the dividend.

▣ where the objective earns its keep — experiments

A north-star plus its counter-metrics isn't just for ranking issues. It's the frame that makes an experiment mean something: when you ship a change and re-run, the objective is what lets you isolate the change's true impact — did the north-star move, and did you trip a constraint doing it? Without a declared objective, an experiment is a number with no verdict.

① Ground — Outcomes

At onboarding, one more question next to "instrument your spans": "how do you know a session worked?" Two paths, one schema — emit an outcome event post-hoc from the system that knows the truth (outcome.name · outcome.status · outcome.value, joined by session.id), or join a warehouse table for orgs that won't re-instrument. The outcome often lands after the agent is done — the agent resolves the chat in seconds; "order delivered / reopened in 72h" is known minutes or days later — so outcomes attach post-hoc, on their own clock. Spec'd as an open OpenInference extension (PRD Appendix A), so it's infrastructure, not lock-in. This is also where every "projected impact" number gets its legitimacy: the business metrics are instrumented, not imagined.

② Lens — the Objective

A 5-minute setup that becomes the product's center of gravity: purpose (one sentence) · north-star (one outcome-derived metric) · constraints (the counter-metrics that must not get worse while chasing it — named "constraints" because Guardrails is an existing AX runtime feature, and this isn't that). Every issue is ranked by projected north-star lift net of constraint risk — always labeled a ceiling, never a point estimate. And the objective is versioned and revisable by the loop itself: when the org declines a recommendation for a reason the objective didn't know ("that quota is a fraud control"), the decline proposes a new constraint. The objective gets truer with every decision.

◆ where this goes — business context for a balanced objective

Setting the objective is the natural place to capture broader business context — what the org actually trades off, what a "win" costs elsewhere. That context is what lets Alyx pressure-test an objective for completeness: is the north-star paired with the right counter-metrics, or is the agent being pushed to optimize one number into a wall? (A direction the record makes possible — not the MVP. V1 just captures purpose, north-star, and constraints.)

the third layer · the engine

Decisions — from recommendation to shipped, owned, and proven.

The unit of value isn't a score, and it isn't even a fix suggestion — it's a decision that gets made. Each issue maps to a change type, and the change type determines the route and the verb:

Change typeExampleRoutes toCTA

The Decision Artifact — built from what we already know

The V1 artifact deliberately leans on data the platform already has, not on intelligence it has to invent: how often is this happening (cluster size, share of volume), what we observe — both the failing evals and the joined outcome — and the tradeoff (north-star upside and the constraint it puts at risk). That compiles into a shareable package routed to an owner, viewable by link without a login (the policy owner isn't an AX user — yet), carrying a status: proposed → routed → decided (shipped / declined / blocked) → verified / deviated, with reasons captured. That status + reason field is the quiet differentiator — what enables or prevents a decision is data nobody collects today.

Explicitly not the MVP: turning this into the org's system of record for policy changes, consent/endorsement tracking, or anything beyond capturing the decision and its reason. That earns its way in once the record exists.

Tool specs as first-class output

When the gap is a missing capability, the output is a drafted, lightweight tool spec — name, trigger conditions, rough I/O shape, evidence traces, and the cross-intent footprint. It starts deliberately thin and gets sharper over time; the point isn't a finished design, it's a scoped, evidenced starting point for engineering. Modality gaps generalize hard: an agent that can't evaluate images or GPS fails dozens of intents that look unrelated symptom-by-symptom. Clustering by missing capability instead of symptom is what makes the spec a roadmap input, not a complaint.

walkthrough · DoorDash support agent

Two issues, two different decisions — both wins.

Setup: Purpose = resolve Dasher support issues autonomously. North-star = resolution rate without escalation, computed from the outcome join (sessions ⟷ case-resolution table). Constraints = erroneous-payout rate (outcome join), cost per contact (trace-derived), undelivered-order rate (outcome join).

Issue 1 · Dashers ask the agent to mark their own order delivered — and it can't change: Policy routes to: policy owner
Trigger
An escalation-necessity eval — would a reasonable agent have needed to hand this off? — flags sessions where the agent reasoned correctly and was still blocked. Correctness evals pass these; the outcome data (order still not marked delivered) exposes them.
Root cause · Alyx
A quota limit, not a model error. 1,240 sessions where a Dasher asked to mark their delivery complete; the agent reasoned correctly but hit a per-window mark_delivered cap → quota_exceeded → forced human handoff. 94% correct reasoning across the cluster.
◆ the decision — routed to the people who already handle these
The platform does not recommend weakening the quota — it's a fraud control it can't adjudicate. It compiles the Decision Artifact and routes it to the policy owner with a concrete ask: have humans review what human agents actually do with these escalations today, to decide whether the quota can be extended for these cases, or whether there are other checkable conditions (verified Dasher, GPS-confirmed at address, prior clean history) that would let the agent mark the order delivered automatically.
ceiling +3.1pt resolution (5% vol × ~62% recoverable) constraint · erroneous payouts flagged — the quota exists for a reason evidence: 1,240 traces ›
Issue 2 · The agent's instructions say "an image needs review" — so it escalates, every time change: Tool · capability gap 14 intents · one missing modality
What symptom-clustering sees
Three unrelated issues, because the photo means something different in each: delivery disputes (is this the right doorstep?), damaged-item claims (what's actually in the photo?), address confirmation.
What capability-clustering sees
One missing modality across 14 intents — 3.2% of weekly volume sits behind a single gap: the agent can't look at the picture, so the escalation rule fires unconditionally.
◆ the decision — a drafted spec for a general photo-evaluation tool
Not one narrow function — a photo-evaluation tool the agent can call, with the spec naming what each case is actually looking for: match against past successful deliveries (same doorstep / scene), identify order size or specific item presence (does the photo show the claimed items?), and an extensible slot for the next photo intent. Routed to engineering with the aggregate footprint — 14 intents, not one symptom.
ceiling +2.2pt across all 14 intents mitigation: low confidence → current escalation path, unchanged spec: PRD Appendix B ›
▣ thirty days later

The photo tool shipped → re-measured → +1.7pt verified (vs. +2.2 ceiling) → decision closed. The quota artifact: the policy owner extended the cap for verified Dashers with a clean history and +2.4pt verified; the remainder stayed escalated by design — reason captured, one constraint added to the objective. Both are wins: one shipped a capability, one made a policy call the platform couldn't make alone and recorded why. That's a decision system, not a suggestion feed.

the mvp

Principles first. Then the cut.

principle 1
Advances a named decision

Every feature moves a decision from open → made → shipped. Insight without a decision attached is out.

principle 2
Downstream of the objective

If it works identically without outcomes + an objective, a competitor ships it without our wedge — out.

principle 3
One agent, one join

Demoable on a single instrumented agent, at most one outcome join. No fleet scale, no accumulated history required.

principle 4
Record before intelligence

V1's job is just to capture the decision record cheaply — status, reason, before/after. Get the substrate first; the smart features that learn from it come later.

The MVP is one full turn of the loop — told straight

Start where the developer already is: Signal hands over clusters of eval failures. The MVP layers the missing context on top of that input, in order:

First, ground and rank it. Attach the agent's objective and the joined outcome to those clusters, so each one is no longer "a failing eval" but "a failing eval that's costing this business metric, this often." That's the step nothing else does.

Then classify the resolution. Each ranked issue gets a change type — prompt, context, tool, permission, policy — and, where the fix lives outside the repo, a "decision required" artifact carrying the tradeoff (north-star upside and the constraint at risk), routed to the owner who can act on it.

Then track whether it actually happened. The artifact's status records if and when a fix shipped — and on post-change traffic the loop re-measures lift in both the eval and the business outcome, closing the decision on verified movement or flagging the deviation. One agent, one join, one full turn — that's the demo.

Out, by principle: attribution timeline + velocity dashboards (need history — V2) · fleet capability rollup (needs >1 agent — V2) · history-shaped recommendations (V3) · any auto-applied fix (trust is earned — V3+) · simulation/replay (V2) · dedicated policy-owner console (the shareable link is the MVP version).

metrics

The point is the business metric — not the eval score.

The whole system would be a failure if it drove eval scores up while the business outcome stayed flat. So the metrics are built to reward moved outcomes, not green dashboards:

TierMetric — and why it's the right one
North-starVerified improvement rate — % of agent projects that ship ≥1 decision through the loop within 30 days and see the declared business north-star move. Not "evals improved" — the outcome improved.
LeadingTime-to-first-decision · decisions reaching a terminal state (declined-with-reason is a success; stalled is the failure) · share of decisions that are higher-order (tool/permission/policy/context, not just prompt) — the differentiator.
Decision record% of decisions that captured a reason + before/after. This is principle 4 made measurable: it's the data the V2/V3 intelligence will learn from, so V1's success is partly just having recorded the decisions cleanly.
Forecast honestyThe projected-vs-verified gap — how far our projected ceilings ran ahead of what actually shipped. We measure it on ourselves so the recommender is forced to get more honest over time, rather than overselling lift.

OSS gets you the raw materials — spans, an objective, even outcome events. But because the outcome arrives late and a single event isn't a recommendation, the moment those signals become a ranked issue, a routed decision, and verified lift is the moment you're in AX. The conversion trigger is no longer "persist an eval," or even "see an issue" — it's "a decision the platform routed got made, and the metric moved."

risks & roadmap

The hard parts — and how we meet them.

Challenge / riskHow we meet it
"This is Signal — we launched it last week."Named up front; Signal is the issue input where available. This layer is what Signal doesn't claim: outcome ground truth, objective ranking, decisions outside the repo, verified close.
Getting teams to instrument outcomes challenge, not riskThe real work, so make it cheap and retrofittable: outcomes don't have to be planned up front — a team can add the join later to extend Signal's reach from "what broke" to "what it cost the business." And even with no outcomes yet — just a described objective — the platform can explore impact conversationally from the evals and traces it already has, then upgrade to hard numbers once an outcome is wired.
Projected lift is fake precisionTrue early — so it's always a labeled ceiling with visible arithmetic, never a point estimate; only verified lift closes a decision; and the projected-vs-verified gap is a tracked metric that forces the number to get better over time.
"Decision tracking is just Jira"The opposite — Jira is the integration. Ideally we know exactly when a fix is pushed (via the Jira/Linear hook), and then the thing we can prove that Jira can't: the failure mode disappears from the traces and the outcome moves. A ticket can't compile its own evidence or close itself on metric movement.
"Constraint" is vagueMake it concrete: a constraint is a counter-metric that must not regress — e.g. for the DoorDash agent, erroneous-payout rate while we chase resolution rate. It's typed at setup as trace-derived or outcome-joined, and the objective can't name an outcome that isn't instrumented — so the setup form makes any blindness visible instead of papering over it.

Roadmap — the loop matures on a trust ladder

The axis is trust, not time: each rung hands the loop more to do on its own — and only after the last rung earned it.

V1 · human-walked
The loop runs once, by hand.
The platform surfaces the issue, compiles the evidence, and routes it — but a human makes every call. The win isn't automation; it's that the decision record starts.
  • surface · compile · route
  • every decision is human
  • the record begins
V2 · the loop remembers
Now it has a past to reason over.
With a history of decisions and their outcomes, the loop gains memory — it can attribute lift, measure its own velocity, and roll up the fleet.
  • attribution timeline
  • fix-to-lift + decision velocity
  • fleet capability rollup
  • recall of stalled decisions
  • simulation before commit
V3 · the loop learns
It earns the right to act.
Recommendations shaped by what this org actually ships; and for the changes Arize owns directly — prompt & config tuning — trusted ones go semi-auto under a policy owner. Autonomy is earned, never assumed — and only ever reaches the tunable layer (see note).
  • org-shaped recommendations
  • semi-auto for tunable changes only
  • policy-owner surface
  • earned autonomy
▲ a ceiling on autonomy — by design

The ladder has a limit the platform structurally can't cross. Arize can draft and, eventually, auto-apply a prompt or config change — that's tuning it owns end-to-end. But the higher-value changes — a new tool, a new permission, added context, a policy call — require the user to act: the platform can compile the decision, route it, and prove the result, but it can never ship it alone. So "earned autonomy" only ever reaches the tunable layer; everything in the discretionary band stays a human decision the platform makes faster and proves. That's not a gap in the roadmap — it's the thesis restated: the changes that matter most live outside the repo, which is exactly why a decision surface, not an auto-fixer, is the right bet.

▣ the build-to-proposal bridge

Building and instrumenting my own agent on AX this week, I had the trace and could see the retrieval failure — but turning that insight into a durable, decided fix was manual and lived in my head. And nothing in my telemetry could say whether the answer my agent gave actually served the user — the outcome lived outside the trace. Both gaps — signal to decision, interaction to outcome — are what this closes. (I built DoorDash's multi-agent support platform on this exact stack — Arize + OpenTelemetry — so I've lived the policy-owner handoff and the outcome blindness as the customer.)

appendix · spec depth

The detail behind the argument.

Folded in so the proposal stays one document — open what you want to inspect. The two specs below are the load-bearing ones: the open convention that makes outcomes portable, and a worked example of the tool spec the engine drafts.

Appendix A · Outcome semantic convention proposed OpenInference extension

A standard way to attach business outcomes to agent sessions, neutral across frameworks — emitted by whichever system learns the truth, whenever it learns it. Attached to a session post-hoc by session.id:

AttributeTypeExampleNotes
outcome.namestringorder.delivered · case.resolveddot-namespaced, org-defined
outcome.statusenumachieved｜failed｜reversed｜unknownreversed covers false resolutions (reopened within window)
outcome.valuedouble1.0 · 42.50optional; unit via outcome.unit
outcome.kindenumnorth_star｜constraint｜informationalhow the objective layer consumes it
outcome.observed_attimestamp—when truth was learned, distinct from ingest time
outcome.sourcestringwarehouse:delivery_factsprovenance
session.idstring—the join key; the one thing the agent must propagate

Three emission paths, one schema: (1) an SDK event from the business system; (2) a batch-ingest endpoint; (3) the warehouse join — a mapping config (session_id_column, outcome_name, status_expr, observed_at_column) that materializes the same attributes with no emission. All three land identically; evals, the objective layer, and decisions consume one schema.

Why a convention, not a feature: the same reason OpenInference exists. If outcome semantics are vendor-proprietary, the loop is a walled garden; if they're an open extension, every framework can emit them and Arize is the place they become an improvement loop. The neutral party is the only one who can credibly propose it.

Appendix B · Worked tool spec drafted from Issue 2

What engineering receives for a capability gap — a scoped, evidenced, prioritized starting point, not "the agent should handle images." Deliberately lightweight; the open_questions_for_eng block is the point: the platform drafts, the owner finishes.

tool: evaluate_delivery_photo
status: draft — generated from Issue #218, edited by [owner]
purpose: >
  Let the agent actually look at a submitted photo and answer what each
  photo intent needs, so image-gated cases resolve without a blind escalation.
trigger_conditions:
  - session intent ∈ {delivery_dispute, damaged_item, wrong_address_claim, ...}  # 14 intents
  - an image attachment is present on the session
modes:                       # what the tool is asked to look for, per intent
  match_prior_delivery:      # delivery disputes — is this the right place?
    compares: submitted photo ↔ past successful-delivery photos for this address
    returns: { match: enum[confirmed, mismatch, indeterminate], confidence }
  inspect_contents:          # damaged / wrong-item claims
    identifies: order size, presence of specific claimed items, visible damage
    returns: { items_present: list, damage: bool, confidence }
  # extensible: a new photo intent adds a mode, not a new tool
inputs:
  image:    { type: binary, source: customer_upload }
  order_id: { type: string, source: session context }
  mode:     { type: enum,   source: intent → mode mapping }
evidence:
  - 412 sessions escalated with image attachments the agent acknowledged
    but could not evaluate (traces linked)
  - cross-intent footprint: 14 intents, 3.2% of weekly volume
projection:
  ceiling: +2.2pt resolution rate (all 14 intents, full recovery — labeled ceiling)
constraint_exposure:
  erroneous_payout_rate: medium — gate auto-resolution on confidence ≥ 0.8
  (below threshold → current escalation path, unchanged)
open_questions_for_eng:
  - reuse an existing CV service, or net-new model per mode?
  - PII handling for customer-uploaded images (retention window?)
Appendix C · Open questions what's deliberately unresolved
  • Outcome-event latency. Outcomes arrive minutes-to-days after the session — what's the staleness window before an issue's ranking is recomputed?
  • Decline-reason capture. Free text + optional tags in V1, or structured from day one? (Leaning free text + LLM-extraction — don't tax the policy owner.)
  • Where the Decision Artifact lives for orgs that demand SSO on everything — link-with-token vs. a lightweight viewer seat.
  • Objective ownership — project or org? (V1: project. Fleet rollup forces the org question in V2.)
