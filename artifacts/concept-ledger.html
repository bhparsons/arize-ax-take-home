appendix · concepts considered for the proposal

The alternatives,
and why the proposal won.

Before landing on From Signal to Decisions, I worked through eight candidate concepts for "what should Arize build to grow AX adoption among developers building agents." This page is that map: each concept, and the one honest answer to "why isn't this the proposal?"

◆ how each concept was tested

One line per concept: pain observed → what Arize / the market already does → what's actually still missing → verdict. Two rules keep it honest:

1 · Name what already exists first. Credibility comes from being specific about what Arize and competitors ship today — not from pretending the field is empty.

2 · "A competitor shipped it" is market evidence, not a verdict. If Langfuse or an open standard solves a piece but Arize doesn't, and the developer has to leave Arize to get it, that fragmentation is a reason to build — not a reason to drop. We don't get a capability "for free" because someone else has it.

The two highest-value gaps below — trusting the evals, and turning high-stakes failures into recorded decisions — are what the proposal is built on. This appendix is the reasoning behind that choice; the handoff says where each thread lands in the proposal itself.

how to read

Every concept lands in one of three places.

◆ the three verdicts

🚀 real gap — Arize doesn't do this, and a developer building agents would be better off if it did. Build it. Either the headline, or a piece that makes the headline complete.

✅ in Arize — but buried — the capability exists, but it's hard to find or only half-works. The fix is discoverability or reliability, not a new build.

🔧 worked around — I hit it during the build and solved it outside the platform. A small DX papercut, and a candidate to make first-party.

the backbone · what evals are actually for

Not every eval failure is a bug to fix.

The concepts below only sort cleanly once you see that evals do different jobs depending on where the failure sits. This is the spine the whole proposal hangs from.

Failures stack on a ladder, and the rung decides what you do about them — patch it, or decide about it:

RungThe questionExample failureWhat you do with it
1 · ReliableDoes it even run?tool errors, malformed output, timeoutsTriage & fix. Not a decision — just broken.
2 · Capable & accurateDoes it do the right thing?missing tool, missing permission, a policy that blocks correct reasoning, hallucinationFrame a decision. A tradeoff, often intentional — needs an owner and a record.
3 · Good experienceIs it a good experience?too verbose, off-tone, misses user frustrationTune — usually a product/design call.
4 · ExploratoryWhat don't we know yet?new intents, drift, unexplained spikesInvestigate.
◆ the move that matters

Low rungs are fixes. High rungs are decisions. Rung-1 failures get triaged and patched — nobody needs to weigh in. Rung-2 is different: the agent hit a boundary (it lacked a tool, a permission, the knowledge, or a policy blocked it), and resolving it is a tradeoff someone has to own and record — not a line an engineer quietly ships.

And the ladder is climbed for a business objective, not an eval score. Which Rung-2 tradeoffs are worth making is set by the agent's North Star — its actual purpose — not by whichever eval happens to be red. The two lead gaps below (trusting the evals, the decision surface) are exactly the pieces missing to make that real — which is why they're what the proposal builds.

★ the gap the proposal headline grew from
real gap · the lead

Trust the evals — is the grader accurate, and is it measuring the right thing?

🚀 real gapthe proposal's headline

Pain: every tool can now generate an eval (an automated grader) from a bad output — none check whether that grader is any good. Two separate questions go unanswered: is it accurate (does it agree with a human, or does it false-alarm), and is it measuring the right thing (is it graded against what the agent is actually for). You're asked to trust a grader you never tested, scoring something you never confirmed matters.

What to build: before a generated eval ships, test it on held-back examples and report whether it still agrees with human reviewers and how often it false-alarms. Activation metric: % of new agent projects with ≥1 persisted, trust-checked AX eval within 7 days.

Why it's the headline: observability tells you what failed; nothing tells you whether the thing measuring failure can be trusted. Every downstream decision in the proposal rests on a number from an eval — so the eval has to be trustworthy first. It also has a natural lifecycle — certify → monitor → decompose, which is where the next two gaps (judge-drift + reviewer agreement) live: certify the grader pre-ship on held-out examples, keep the agreement check running post-ship and alert on decay, and break the score down per rubric criterion to localize the cause.

market scan & evidence verdict: open sliver, closing fast · 2026-06-11

Tighten the claim. "Calibrate a judge against human labels" is now table stakes (LangSmith, Galileo, Patronus, Braintrust all ship it). The unoccupied sliver is specifically held-out replay + overfit / false-positive-rate guard as a productized step — not judge calibration in general.

① At Arize — open. Arize has the narrative but not the surface: its flagship blog literally prescribes the loop ("re-run on the same set and a holdout set," names Cohen's kappa + confusion matrices), and it publicly engaged the exact thesis in its EvalGen "Who Validates the Validators" breakdown — but ships only the primitives (annotations, Phoenix Evals), no first-class eval-of-eval / held-out-replay product. (judge blog)

② Best practice to cite. Shankar et al., "Who Validates the Validators?" (EvalGen, UIST 2024) — names "criteria drift" (criteria overfit to outputs seen), the precise risk held-out replay catches. Methodology: human-labeled validation set → judge↔human agreement via Cohen's / weighted kappa (human ceiling κ≈0.80) → confusion matrix → bias correction → retest on holdout. The false-positive-rate guard is the second half.

③ Closest competitor — LangSmith "Align Evals" (Jul 29 2025). Owns "score your evaluator" (live alignment score, baseline compare, sorted unaligned cases) — but optimizes on the labeled set with no held-out split or overfit warning. That sliver is genuinely unoccupied; the surrounding space is not. (changelog)

real gap · what high-rung failures actually need

The decision surface — a Rung-2 failure is a decision to record, not a code fix.

🚀 real gapthe proposal's decision engine

Pain: when an agent hits a Rung-2 boundary — a policy blocks correct reasoning, a missing permission forces a handoff, a capability gap — the resolution is a tradeoff someone has to own and record, and that someone is often not the engineer (a policy owner, a platform team, legal). There's no surface where that decision gets framed, routed, signed off, and kept. Today it's a screenshot in Slack: the recommendation exists; the decision never gets recorded, and nothing tracks why. Lived evidence: DoorDash built internal tooling that "skinned" Arize so operators could manage prompt versions and configure evals on their side, passing through to Arize where the evals ran. It "felt sloppy" — because the surface they needed didn't exist, so they hand-built it.

What to build: a decision record for high-rung failures — the issue, the evidence, the tradeoff (the upside and what it costs), the owner it routes to, and a status trail (proposed → routed → decided → verified). Viewable by the non-engineer owner without an Arize login, because they don't have one yet.

Why it's a gap, not plumbing: the eval-config data round-trip already ships (the Online Tasks GraphQL API), so this isn't about wiring. What's missing is the surface where a tradeoff becomes an owned, recorded decision — the thing the ladder says Rung-2 failures need and nothing provides. In the proposal this is the Decision Artifact: the engine that turns a trusted eval signal into a decision that actually gets made.

market scan & evidence verdict: gap real, but must differentiate · 2026-06-11

Sharpen the wedge. "Non-technical prompt approvals" is already owned by competitors — so this can't be approvals-in-the-abstract. The defensible wedge is the operator console that round-trips to Arize's eval runtime (configure on your side → execute inside Arize), not approvals per se.

The data round-trip IS first-class. The Online Tasks GraphQL API exposes createEvalTask / patchEvalTask / runOnlineTask (config: modelId, samplingRate, queryFilter, templateEvaluators, llmConfig, runContinuously) — but mutations are enterprise-gated. The policy-owner view / approval UX is NOT first-class — teams hand-roll it (the DoorDash skin). Gap remaining: the engineer-facing config plumbing exists, but there's no first-party surface where a non-engineer policy owner reviews, approves, and signs off on eval/prompt changes with an audit trail.

① At Arize — open (no native approvals). AX ships granular RBAC + SSO and immutable Prompt Hub versions/tags, but RBAC ≠ approvals and tags ≠ sign-off — no maker-checker state machine. The "AI Factory" blog adds an engineer-only propose→review→approve loop on agent-proposed PRs — not a non-technical governance console. The banking blog cites "workflows for non-technical stakeholders" but means visibility, not change-management. Not on any public roadmap found.

② Best practice to cite. Maker-checker / segregation of duties (SR 11-7 model-risk lineage — note SR 11-7 was replaced by a principles-based framework on 2026-04-17; the SoD pattern persists). The proven config-approval UX to copy is LaunchDarkly: per-environment required approvals, PR-style review, tag-scoped enforcement. GitOps protected-branch review is the engineer-native equivalent.

③ Closest competitors. Humanloop = closest analog (non-technical prompt editing + deployment-approval workflows) — but it shut down Sept 2025, acquired by Anthropic, leaving a hole. Braintrust = strongest eval-config governance (RBAC over who can approve scorers/datasets + CI release gates). PromptLayer = RBAC + approval + audit for regulated teams. Credo AI = governs systems/risk, not prompt/eval configs (adjacent). None round-trip to an external eval runtime — that's the daylight.

real gaps · what makes trust-the-evals complete

Two more gaps — and why "a competitor has it" is a reason to build, not skip.

These two live inside the trust-the-evals lifecycle: certify the grader pre-ship, then monitor it on live traffic, and decompose a stuck score to find the cause. Pieces of each exist at other vendors — Langfuse, Label Studio. That's the problem, not the answer: a developer who has to leave Arize to check whether their own grader still works has a broken loop. Owning these is what makes the eval trustworthy end-to-end, on one surface.

Live judge-drift — does the grader still agree with humans?

🚀 real gap · the monitor beat

Pain: a grader certified today drifts. On live traffic the grader's label and a human's land on the same span, but nothing tracks where they diverge over time or alerts when agreement decays below the bar it was certified at.

Why build it anyway: Langfuse shipped the comparison view (kappa, agreement-over-time) in Nov 2025 — but it's in Langfuse. Arize has no live agreement monitor, so a team tracing in Arize must wire up a second tool to know its grader still works. That surface-switch is the gap. In the proposal this is the monitor beat: keep the certified agreement check running post-ship and alert on decay.

market scan 2026-06-12

① At Arize. Annotations are a manual way to find judge↔human disagreement; online evals emit only a label per span; Align Evals does the comparison offline, pre-ship, on a calibration set. No live agreement time-series, no drift alert.

② Best practice. Use Cohen's kappa, not correlation — correlation hides systematic bias ("Judge's Verdict," Oct 2025). Judges drift on a ~60–90 day horizon, so agreement has to be re-measured continuously.

③ The market. Langfuse Score Analytics (2025-11-07) ships live human-vs-judge comparison with kappa + over-time tracking. LangSmith Align Evals is one-time static calibration and names drift as roadmap. Threshold alerting on decay is unoccupied — and belongs inside Arize's trust-the-evals loop, not bolted on from a second vendor.

Honest caveat: live agreement needs a steady stream of fresh human labels, which many teams don't generate at volume — so in practice this leans on the same held-out set, sampled over time.

Reviewer agreement — do the humans even agree?

🚀 real gap · the decompose beat

Pain: when an eval won't settle, you can't tell noisy human labels from a weak grader prompt. AX labeling queues send each item to one reviewer and never show whether reviewers agree or where they split — so "the grader is wrong" and "the humans disagree" look identical.

Why build it anyway: inter-annotator agreement is well-trodden — Label Studio, Argilla, Prodigy all ship it, Langfuse computes it between score sources. But none of that is in Arize, where the grader lives. The piece genuinely missing everywhere: breaking a low agreement score down per rubric criterion to localize which sub-criterion is dragging the grader — the decompose beat.

market scan 2026-06-12

① At Arize. Human annotations have no multi-annotator-per-item or agreement metric; labeling queues actively dedupe — built to send each item to one reviewer, the opposite of overlap-for-agreement. No decomposition feature, though the judge blog already advises "correctness, grounding, safety… should be separate evals."

② Best practice. IAA: Artstein & Poesio, "Inter-Coder Agreement" (2008); pair with Dawid–Skene / MACE for per-annotator reliability. Decomposition: Shankar et al., EvalGen (UIST 2024) names "criteria drift"; analytic rubrics beat holistic single-score judges.

③ The market. Label Studio, Argilla, Prodigy ship IAA; Langfuse computes it in-platform. Per-criterion decomposition tied to the grader is the unoccupied piece.

▣ the lifecycle, for the record

Together with held-out replay, these make trust-the-evals a lifecycle, not a one-shot check: certify the grader pre-ship on held-out examples → monitor agreement post-ship and alert on decay → decompose the score per criterion to localize the cause. "Agreement" = Cohen's / weighted kappa against a certified threshold.

real gap · supporting · I hit this in the build

Portable prompt optimization — optimize your prompt without surrendering it to Prompt Hub.

🚀 real gap · supportingpairs with trust-the-evals

Pain: AX's polished prompt-optimization path wants the prompt to live in Prompt Hub — coupling that sits in tension with Arize's OTel/OpenInference neutrality & portability brand. Getting the prompt into AX is the realest switching cost the platform has. I hit this directly: I kept my prompt in-repo as source of truth and synced a view-only mirror into AX, verifying it doesn't break experiments, Playground, or online-evals. The only forfeit is Hub-native versioning, which the build didn't need.

What to build: run the optimizer on your in-repo prompt against eval feedback and write the improved prompt back as a reviewable diff / PR — hosting optional. No competitor ships that exact motion as one integrated step.

Why it earns a spot — and why it's supporting, not the headline: it's the strongest defensibility play in the set, but by gravity, not lock-in. The pull to manage prompts inside Arize shouldn't come from coupling — it should come from the fact that the evals are meaningful and tied to the business outcome, which is the headline gap. Make the eval loop the best place to know whether a prompt change actually helped, and the prompt (and its variants and experiments) follows the loop home on its own. That's why it pairs natively with Trust the evals: "is the optimized prompt actually better when tested on examples it wasn't built from?"

market scan & evidence verdict: reframe to the write-back · 2026-06-11

The claim moves. "Optimize a raw-string prompt" is already shipped — so the wedge isn't raw strings. It's: an eval-driven optimizer that writes the improved prompt back to your repo as a reviewable diff/PR, no Hub hosting. That exact motion is genuinely unoccupied.

① At Arize — claims verified, with nuance. Raw-string SDK input = CONFIRMED (Prompt-Learning SDK takes a string / message list / PromptVersion). "OpenAI-only" = partially true — default model_choice="gpt-4" and docs list only OpenAI, but the open-source repo has an undocumented provider hook that bypasses the OpenAI default. Git/diff/PR write-back: does NOT exist anywhere in Arize — the beta UI path runs off Hub and saves back as immutable Hub versions. No portable-optimization roadmap found.

② State of the art = code-native. DSPy (MIPROv2, BootstrapFewShot) compiles + save()s the optimized program into your codebase, no hosted registry; OPRO / APE / TextGrad all operate on prompts as in-code parameters. Hosting in a vendor registry is a product choice, not a technical requirement — exactly the tension this names.

③ Closest competitor — Braintrust. braintrust pull/push = bidirectional prompt↔git sync + PR evals that gate merges; Loop auto-optimizes. But even Braintrust treats its registry as home and syncs to git — no tool found runs an optimizer and emits an automatic PR diff against your in-repo prompt. That specific motion is open, and lands squarely on Arize's neutrality/portability brand. (git sync)

in Arize — but buried or half-working · a find/fix, not a build

Already in Arize — the discoverability & reliability fixes.

Two concepts I considered turned out to already exist in Arize — so the honest move isn't to pitch them, it's to name what's actually wrong: you can't find it, or it doesn't reliably work. That's a cheaper fix than a new build, and being straight about it is part of what makes the real gaps above credible.

Eval Copilot — generate an eval from a bad trace

✅ in Arize · via Alyx

The originating gap: a developer looking at a bad trace wants to turn it into a guarding eval without leaving their flow. Real need — it's why I considered it.

What's actually there: Alyx's Eval Builder does this today — but two things hold it back, and I hit both. It's unreliable (it failed twice when I asked it to create an eval during the build), and it's undiscoverable (you have to already know it's there to invoke it). The capability ships; the experience doesn't.

Verdict: not a new build — a reliability + discoverability fix. Make the eval-from-trace path land reliably, and surface it where the developer is already staring at the failure. The deeper residue it can't answer — is the generated eval any good? — isn't this concept; that's Trust the evals.

Variant segmentation — compare prompt / tool / agent variants in one view

✅ in Arize · custom attribute

The originating gap: you change a prompt, a tool, or the agent graph, and you want every eval / cost / latency view to split by which variant produced each trace — without standing up a separate experiment each time.

What's actually there: stamp a custom attribute on the span (the docs call it an "A/B variant") and pivot on it — Feb-2026 Pivot Table widgets plus full-agent experimentation already cover this. (Arize also deliberately doesn't route production traffic, which is the right call.) So the capability is reachable; it's just buried in the docs.

Verdict: a discoverability fix, not a build — make the "tag a variant, segment by it" pattern obvious in the UI. Worth noting it works even with prompts kept fully outside Arize, which quietly weakens Prompt-Hub lock-in as a moat — and supports the gravity argument under portable optimization.

worked around · solved outside the platform during the build

The one I just worked around.

Silent $0 cost

🔧 worked around · DX fix

Pain: cost columns show $0 — easy to mistake for "free." OpenRouter slugs (anthropic/claude-sonnet-4) don't match AX's ~63 built-in price configs, so the per-token match fails silently. The sneakiest friction of the build — the other papercuts announce themselves; this one doesn't.

What I did: added custom cost configs per OpenRouter model slug (Settings → Cost Tracking, cost-per-million-tokens) so spend renders.

Verdict: a small DX fix to make first-party — a "$0 / unmatched slug" warning plus auto-mapping for known gateways (OpenRouter etc.). Not a strategic gap; just a papercut every team behind a gateway hits and hand-rolls the same way.

existing solution & gap remaining

Custom cost configurations are supported; they apply only to future traces. Gap remaining: the platform doesn't warn that a model slug failed to match a price config (silent $0), and doesn't auto-map common gateway slugs — so every team hits this and hand-rolls the same fix.

the handoff · where this lands in the proposal

Where it goes in the proposal.

This page is the reasoning; From Signal to Decisions is the build. The two lead gaps each carry a thread straight into it.

▣ where each gap lands

Trust the evals → the verified-lift close. The proposal closes a decision only on verified movement of the business metric, and labels every projection a ceiling, not a point estimate — the same instinct (don't trust a number you haven't tested), moved up from the eval to the outcome.

The decision surface → decision routing. The proposal's Decision Artifact routes capability / policy / permission tradeoffs to the non-engineer owner who actually decides them, viewable via a login-less link, with a status trail — the Rung-2 "decide and record" move, made real.
